Mars Rover Explorer

For nearly two decades NASA has explored the surface of Mars robotically using rovers, sending back amazing data and images to earth for further study. Using this map, you will learn about three of NASA's rovers - Spirit, Opportunity, and Curiosity - as you explore the surface of Mars from the comfort of your home on Earth.

Endeavor Rim

This image was taken on Dec 11, 2014 and captures Opportunity’s robotic arm examining bedrock in the crater. Of interest to researchers was how these rocks got there and if they could have possibly been transported by water in the past.

Knudsen Ridge

This photo shows Knudsen Ridge which is located on the rim of Marathon Valley. Of interest to NASA was areas known as red zones. These areas have smaller material that can be analyzed by the rover to help discover the presence of past water on the Red Planet. Opportunity showed amazing climbing skills scaling slopes as much as 20 percent in its mission to Knudsen Ridge.

Marathon Valley

This photo shows Marathon Valley which is located within the Endeavor Crater. This valley is named after the milestone that was reached by Opportunity traveling over 26 miles on Mars surface which is equivalent to a marathon race.

Rocheport

This picture shows the western rim of the Endeavor Crater from images taken on Feb 25, 2017. The perpendicular areas that are carved out in the ridge were of special interest to researchers since they are believed to have formed by erosion from either ice, water, or wind.

Orion

This photo is of the Orion Crater which is named after the Apollo 16 lunar mission. Orion Crater is a relatively new feature on the surface of Mars estimated to be only 10 million years old. Orion Crater at 90 feet in width is much smaller than the Endeavor Crater which is near 14 miles wide.

Perseverance Valley

This image from Perseverance Valley shows Opportunity’s tracks using images from its navigation camera. Perseverance Valley is the last location on Opportunity’s 15-year journey. NASA lost communication with Opportunity on June 10, 2018 after a massive dust storm hit the area.

Husband Hill

This 360 panorama view of the “Inner Basin” was taken from Husband Hill over the course of Sols 672-677 and is made up of 405 images. The images are color filtered to enhance differences in terrain features. Husband Hill is visible on the right and left sides of the panorama, with McCool and Ramon Hills rising from the horizon in the center.

Lorre Ridge

This image was taken on Sol 731 in the basin south of Husband Hill. The image is evidence that Lorre Ridge, along with other landforms in the basin, is made up of basaltic rocks (solidified lava) similar to those found on earth. These look to be vesicular rocks, formed by trapped gas bubbles which left holes in the rock as lava solidified around the gas. These geologic findings could tell scientists more about how this area formed.

Winter Haven

Winter Haven was so named because Spirit spent a Martian winter parked on it’s slope so that it remained at an angle where its solar panels would be kept at optimal warmth to “survive” the -100 degree Celsius temperatures. The images composing this panorama, known as the McMurdo panorama, were taken over a series of months between Sol 814-934. The panorama captures great detail in the rover’s surroundings, from volcanic rocks, meteorites, and the “El Dorado” sand dune field to the rock patterns of Home Plate.

Engineering Flats

This spot was named Engineering Flats after Spirit underwent four sols (Martian days) of repairs here on its right front wheel, in addition to other maintenance. It is located in the “Columbia Hills”. Also visible in this image is Hank’s Hollow, where the rover also spent some time.

Home Plate

This rock formation is a part of Home Plate, a location unique for its layered rocks, which can tell scientists a lot about patterns of alternating erosion and deposition here, as well as how the area was formed. It is speculated that these rocks formed after a volcanic explosion and were then altered over time by wind.

High Dune

This photo was taken with Curiosity’s Mastcam on November 27, 2015, during the Sol 1176 of its time on Mars. Pictured is High Dune, a part of the Bagnold Dunes on Mount Sharp and the first dune on Mars to be studied at close range. The dunes are constantly changing, and can move up to a meter each year.

Gale Crater

Curiosity’s path may seem surprisingly short in miles. This is its traverse path up to Sol 1596, or its 1596th Martian day. Up until this point it had driven 9.56 miles (15.39 kilometers). During Sol 1597 (one Martian day later), it encountered this dust devil in Gale Crater. Dust devils on Mars, like those on Earth, are caused by rising heat from the ground. The inset animation in this image shows an accelerated sequence of images taken by Curiosity.
